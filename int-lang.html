The Lakota Language

What is happening with Lakota in education

Within the Lakota language revitalization efforts, steps are being taken to not only revitalize Lakȟótiyapi, but also other Indigenous languages such as the Plains Indian Sign Language (PISL). Teaching Lakȟótiyapi requires decolonization of pedagogy and leaders in the revitalization of this language have incorporated traditional practices into thier teaching such as story-telling. Most importantly language learning is made to be enjoyable!

A big part of the revitalization efforts within the Lakota language is creating a safe-space for learners where trauma is recognized and mistakes are not corrected with judgement.

(Twitchell, 2021, episode 4)

What is happening institutionally


In working to revitalize Lakȟótiyapi, the Lakota Language Consortium (LLC) was established. The LLC is a non-profit organization aids in all things concerned with the education and spread of the Lakota language including teacher trainings.

The LLC also hosts events and keeps an archive dedicated to preserving and storing recordings of the Lakota language.

("Lakota Language Consortium," n.d.)

The Scottish Gaelic Language

What is happening with Gaelic in Scotland's education systems

Though still a part of the United Kingdom, Scotland retains many of it's traditional cultural practices despite the colonization of Scotland by Britain. Revitalization efforts for one of Scotlands Indigenous languages, Gaelic, have started to gain traction in schools across the country.

Scotland instituted an integrated school system of Gaelic-Medium Education (GME) that starts off learners at a young age with basic level Gaelic proficiency. This gives students the option of incorporating the Gaelic language into their education. GME is available in ~60 primary schools across Scotland and has yet to be an option for every student in the Nation.

("Gaelic medium education," n.d.)
Learn more about Gaelic Medium Education

What is happening institutionally with Gaelic in Scotland

In 2003, the United Kingdom recognized Gaelic as an official language of the UK.

In 2005, Bòrd na Gàidhlig was established as part of the Scottish government and tasked with the development and revitalization efforts surrounding the Gaelic language in Scotland.

Since then, there has been a rapid increase in the number of Gaelic speakers, and the production of Gaelic media and radio stations across Scotland.

(Dunmore, 2019)

Te reo Māori

What is happening with Te reo Māori Education

During the late 20th century a philosophy called Kaupapa Māori started to emerge in New Zealand that prioritized the revitalization of Te reo Māori.

Language nests for the Māori language where developed as an alternative to working through the existing school systems. Events for pre-school aged youth to engage with their Indigenous language and culture were introduced by Māori communities.

Kura Kaupapa Māori medium state schools were established with the goal of teaching not only the Māori language, but also traditional world views and emphasized the inncorporation of traditional ecological knowledge (TEK).

(Rameka & Stagg Peterson,2021)

What is happening with Te reo Māori institutionally

Starting in the late 20th century, New Zealand re-shaped their Department of Education aboloshing many over-arching institutions and giving more decision-making power to individual schools.

Despite this, schools in Māori communities were not recieving appropraite funding and there was a lack of standards in education among the individual schools.

Māori communties urged for a new policy in the New Zealand government that would allow for a secondary school system seperate froom the existing one that would govern schools developed and taught in Te reo Māori. By 2009 73 Kura Kaupapa Māori medium state schools were opened, teaching Te reo Māori and using the language in every day instruction.

(Al-Mahrooqi & Asante, 2012)

Lakota

The Lakota language revitalization efforts have seen success with the creation of the Lakota Language Consortium that teaches teachers of the Lakota language and provides materials, events, and archived data to allow learners access to learning the language. These revitalization efforts have also found success in creating safe and enjoyable learnninng environments that allow for multi-lingual approaches that cater to different types of learners from spoken language to sign.

Gaelic

The Gaelic language revitalization efforts in Scotland have seen success through the establishment of a specialized sector in the government tasked with protecting and developing Gaelioc language use across Scotland successfully creating Gaelic medium schools and introducing Gaelic media across Scotland.

Te reo Māori

The Te reo Māori language revitalization efforts in new Zealand have seen success by working within their own commmunites to create schools and programs designed to fully saturate young community memebers in the Indigneous language and get Te reo Māori back to a conversational level.
